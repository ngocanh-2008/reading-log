Article Review: "Why double-majors might beat you out of a job"

1. Pre-Reading Analysis

The article explores the competitive advantage of students who pursue two academic disciplines simultaneously. Before reading, one might expect the advantage to be purely academic (e.g., higher GPA or subject mastery). However, the central hypothesis is that the act of navigating two distinct fields fosters a specific cognitive flexibility—innovation—that is highly coveted by modern employers. The key purpose of the text is to bridge the gap between academic choices and workforce readiness.

2. In-Text Evidence Log

  • Quantitative Advantage: The researchers found that double majors scored 17.4 percentile points higher on innovation measures than their peers.
  • The "Innovation Premium": This advantage remains significant even when controlling for variables like GPA, family history, and even innate personality traits (like extroversion), suggesting the academic pathway itself is the catalyst.
  • Employer Demand: The authors highlight that modern employers value "soft" innovative skills—such as networking across silos, persuasive communication, and working on diverse teams—over traditional grades.
  • The Self-Selection Factor: The authors acknowledge that a desire to double-major may signal that a student is already proactive or creative. This suggests that the double-major experience is both a result of an innovative mindset and a method for further developing it.

3. Post-Reading Reflections

The article challenges the traditional "specialization-only" model of higher education. It suggests that while deep expertise is necessary, the ability to synthesize information from two different fields is what truly creates an innovator. A critical takeaway is that "innovation" is not an innate gift but a set of capacities (networking, risk-taking, communication) that can be cultivated. The authors successfully shift the conversation from what you study to how you study, arguing that the struggle of balancing two disparate disciplines serves as a "real-world" training ground for the complexity of the modern workplace.

4. My Growth and Goals

Reflective Growth: My perspective on academic planning has shifted. I now view the addition of a second major or an interdisciplinary minor not just as an extra workload, but as a strategic investment in my cognitive flexibility and professional "innovation score."

Goals for Further Study:

  • Goal 1: To seek out "interdisciplinary integration" in my current coursework, looking for ways to apply concepts from my elective courses to my core major projects.
  • Goal 2: To prioritize networking with peers and faculty outside my primary department to emulate the interdisciplinary "connective" experiences cited by the authors.

Skill Development: I aim to cultivate the specific innovative traits mentioned—particularly "persuasive communication" and "risk-taking"—by volunteering for cross-functional group projects where I can practice communicating technical concepts to non-experts.

Article Review: "Entertain Me!" by Barbara Hagen

1. Pre-Reading Analysis

The article "Entertain Me!" examines the rapid evolution of American leisure during the 1920s. Before reading, one might expect this piece to focus on how post-WWI economic prosperity provided the necessary capital for households to invest in new, "frivolous" technologies. The core premise is that the Roaring 20s represented a technological tipping point where entertainment shifted from localized, live performances to centralized, mechanized media that could be broadcast or projected to the masses.

2. In-Text Evidence Log

  • The Rise of Radio: Hagen identifies radio as the first "mechanized entertainment" for the home. It moved from military utility to a domestic staple, creating a communal experience where entire families consumed news, drama, and comedy together.
  • The Hollywood System: The author details the birth of the "Big Five" studio system (including MGM, Warner Brothers, and 20th Century Fox). This concentration of power in Southern California allowed for the first standardized production of "celebrity" culture, featuring icons like Charlie Chaplin and Mary Pickford.
  • The Transition of Film: A major turning point occurred with the introduction of sound. The success of The Jazz Singer (1927) and the debut of Steamboat Willie (1928) signaled the end of the silent film era and the birth of modern, character-driven animation.
  • Early Television: The article concludes by noting that by the late 1920s, experimental television—despite its low-quality, grainy output—laid the foundation for the visual dominance that would eventually replace radio as the primary medium in the American home.

3. Post-Reading Reflections

The shift documented by Hagen is not just about gadgets; it is about a fundamental change in human behavior. In the 1920s, the "communal experience"—gathering around a single radio or visiting a local theater—was a requirement of the technology. Today, our media is fragmented and personal. Furthermore, the "Big Five" studio system illustrates a classic economic model of vertical integration, where the same companies that produced the films also owned the theaters that exhibited them. This created a controlled cultural narrative that influenced fashion, language, and societal expectations across the entire nation, effectively creating a "national identity" for the first time.

4. My Growth and Goals

Reflective Growth: My understanding of the 1920s has shifted from viewing the decade solely as a time of economic excess to seeing it as a crucial "media revolution." I now recognize that our modern obsession with screens has deep roots in the 1920s' desire to bring the world into the living room.

Goals for Further Study:

  • Goal 1: To research how the "Big Five" studio system eventually faced antitrust legislation, which mirrors modern debates about media monopolies.
  • Goal 2: To compare the "Golden Age of Radio" with the current "Golden Age of Podcasts," exploring why audiences return to audio-only storytelling despite having access to high-definition video.

Skill Development: I aim to improve my ability to synthesize historical technological shifts with present-day societal trends, ensuring that my analysis remains grounded in the context of the era being studied.

Article Review: "What's wrong with our food system" by Birke Baehr

1. Pre-Reading Analysis

Birke Baehr’s talk addresses the disconnect between the "happy farm" imagery marketed to children and the reality of industrial food production. Before reading, one might expect this piece to be a simplified overview; however, the text serves as a stark critique of modern agribusiness. The core premise is that consumers—especially youth—are being misled by corporate marketing, and that a transition to organic, local farming is necessary for both personal health and environmental preservation.

2. In-Text Evidence Log

IssueDescriptionProposed Solution
Genetically Engineered SeedsManipulating DNA (e.g., fish genes in tomatoes) in labs.Avoiding processed foods; supporting organic growers.
Soil DegradationMonocropping and reliance on fossil-fuel-based fertilizers.Crop rotation and organic soil management.
Chemical RunoffWidespread use of pesticides and herbicides poisoning water.Elimination of synthetic chemical inputs.
Industrial Feeding (CAFOs)Confined, unnatural conditions for livestock.Supporting pasture-raised, local livestock farming.

3. Post-Reading Reflections

Baehr’s argument hinges on a powerful dichotomy: "pay the farmer or pay the hospital." He frames the higher upfront cost of organic food not as an expense, but as a preventative healthcare investment. A critical reflection here is the role of "transparency." Baehr argues that if children truly understood where their food came from—the reality of the "dark side" of the industry—they would naturally reject unhealthy, highly processed alternatives. He suggests that the "food movement" is gaining momentum through community-based solutions like farmers' markets and direct relationships with local producers.

4. My Growth and Goals

Reflective Growth: My understanding of the food system has moved beyond simple nutrition to include the environmental and economic ethics of production. I now realize that consumer choices act as a direct vote for the type of agricultural system we want to support.

Goals for Further Study:

  • Goal 1: To investigate the feasibility of "local-first" eating in urban environments where farmers' markets may be less accessible.
  • Goal 2: To learn more about the scientific debate surrounding Genetically Modified Organisms (GMOs) to form a balanced perspective on the role of technology in food security versus health risks.

Skill Development: I aim to become a more conscious consumer by reading food labels more closely and identifying which products in my diet are linked to industrialized supply chains versus local sources.

Article Review: "Training the GOAT"

1. Pre-Reading Analysis

Before reading, the title "Training the GOAT" clearly points toward a focus on excellence, longevity, and high-performance standards in professional sports. The reader should approach this text expecting to learn how Tom Brady maintained such a long career in a physically demanding sport. The core inquiry is whether Brady’s success is due to traditional athletic training or the alternative, holistic methods championed by Alex Guerrero.

2. In-Text Evidence Log

FeatureTraditional Athletic TrainingAlex Guerrero (TB12 Method)
Primary GoalInjury rehabilitation and recovery.Injury prevention ("Pre-hab").
PhilosophyWorks with MDs; evidence-based Western medicine.Holistic, Eastern-influenced; "Pliability."
TechniqueStandard medical protocols (e.g., RICE for injuries).Massage, meditation, and early re-education of muscle.
CredentialsBachelor’s or Master’s degrees in human anatomy/physiology.Traditional Chinese medicine (SAMRA University).

3. Post-Reading Reflections

The article highlights a fundamental tension in sports medicine: the divide between conventional Western medical practice and holistic, alternative approaches. Guerrero’s "pliability" focus—replacing bulky muscle mass with soft, flexible tissue—is the cornerstone of his methodology. However, the text does not ignore the controversy. Critics point out a lack of traditional scientific validation for Guerrero's methods and question his unconventional educational background. The author leaves the reader to grapple with a compelling question: Does the anecdotal evidence of Brady’s unparalleled longevity outweigh the lack of peer-reviewed, traditional medical consensus?

4. My Growth and Goals

Reflective Growth: I have gained a deeper understanding of how "non-traditional" approaches can gain traction in high-stakes environments when they produce visible results. I now see that "training" can be as much about psychological and nutritional management as it is about physical exercise.

Goals for Further Study:

  • Goal 1: To research the scientific definition of "muscle pliability" to understand if there is a modern physiological equivalent to Guerrero’s term.
  • Goal 2: To compare the career lengths of professional quarterbacks who utilize traditional team training versus those who hire private, personalized "body coaches."

Skill Development: I aim to improve my critical evaluation skills by distinguishing between results-based arguments (Brady’s performance) and methodology-based arguments (the scientific critiques of Guerrero).

Article Review: "Can Television Be Considered Literature and Taught in English Classes?"

1. Pre-Reading Analysis

The article examines the tension between traditional literacy and modern media. The title presents a provocative question that challenges the boundaries of "canonized" literature. The reader should approach this text expecting a balanced debate: one that acknowledges the artistic sophistication of modern television while defending the unique cognitive benefits of the written word.

2. In-Text Evidence Log

PerspectiveKey ArgumentsSupporting Evidence
The Case for TV"Golden Era" TV shows are complex, serialized, and culturally significant.The rise of complex plots (e.g., Breaking Bad, The Wire) mirrors the depth of novels.
The Case Against TVTelevision is visual, not textual; it may lack the cognitive rigor of reading.Tohoku University study linked high TV consumption to lower verbal scores.
Cultural ValueTV acts as a modern "binding" force for a fractured culture.The urban-rural divide in viewership (e.g., Girls vs. Duck Dynasty) shows how media impacts social cohesion.
The Middle GroundUse critical writing about television as a bridge to literacy.Using recaps and cultural criticism allows for analysis without sacrificing reading practice.

3. Post-Reading Reflections

Ostergaard’s analysis identifies a crucial shift: American culture is no longer viewing the same stories. While novels like The Scarlet Letter once provided a common cultural touchstone, television viewership is now highly segmented by geography and demographics. However, the author stops short of recommending that television replace literature. Instead, she highlights the "National Endowment for the Arts" data showing a decline in literary reading—from 56.9% of adults in 1982 to 43.1% in 2015. This suggests that while TV is a valid subject for study, the classroom has an increasingly vital role in preserving the specific cognitive benefits—such as increased language connectivity in the brain—that only deep reading can provide.

4. My Growth and Goals

Reflective Growth: I have learned to distinguish between "entertainment" and "complex literature" within media. I now see that the medium itself (a book vs. a screen) matters less than the complexity of the narrative and the quality of the critical engagement required by the audience.

Goals for Further Study:

  • Goal 1: To explore how "literary criticism" of television (the "middle ground" proposed by the author) can be used to teach analytical writing and argumentation.
  • Goal 2: To examine the pedagogical potential of pairing a classic novel with a modern serialized television show to contrast how each medium handles themes like morality, character development, and social justice.

Skill Development: I aim to improve my ability to apply formal literary analysis techniques (usually reserved for novels) to visual media, such as analyzing camera work as a form of "narrative voice."
